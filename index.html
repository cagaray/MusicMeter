An Understanding of Musical Instruments


A musical instrument is an instrument created or adapted to make musical sounds. In principle, any object that produces sound can be a musical instrument—it is through purpose that the object becomes a musical instrument.

The history of musical instruments dates to the beginnings of human culture. Early musical instruments may have been used for ritual, such as a trumpetto signal success on the hunt, or a drum in a religious ceremony.

The oldest object that some scholars refer to as a musical instrument, a simple flute, dates back as far as 67,000 years (disputed accounts)

  • 1690

    Clarinet

    Invented by Johann Christoph Denner of Nuremburg, Clarinet is a single-reed woodwind instrument with a cylindrical tube.

  • 1720

    Piano

    The first piano was invented in Florence, Italy in 1700 by Bartolomeo Cristofori,a craftsman who repaired harpsichords for Italy’s royal court.

  • 1814

    Saxophone

    The saxophone was invented by Antoine-Joseph Sax. It i usually made of brass and played with a single-reed mouthpiece.

  • 1830

    Flute

    It produces sound when a stream of air is directed against the edge of a hole, causing the air within the body of the instrument to vibrate. It was invented by the German instrument maker Theobald Bohm.

  • 1850

    Guitar

    Guitar has a flat-backed rounded body that narrows in the middle, a long fretted neck, and usually six strings, played by strumming or plucking.The modern classical guitar is credited to Antonio Torres.

  • 1909

    Drum set

    William F. Ludwig and his brother Theobald made history, revolutionizing drumming with the invention of the first spring driven pedal.

  • 1909

    Synthesizer

    Canadian, Hugh Le Caine built the world's first voltage controlled music synthesizer, touch sensitive keyboard, and variable speed multi-track tape recorder.

Types of Instruments

Musical instruments are grouped into different types primarily based on how sound is produced. However, not every instrument fits neatly in a group, as some play a dual role.

For example, a piano has strings that vibrate, and hammers that strike. Is it a string instrument or a percussion instrument? Some say it is both!

Wind

Musical instruments in which sound is produced by the vibration of air, typically by the player blowing into the instrument.
The notes are produced by manipulatng the air columns.
Broadly classified into brass and wind instruments based on how sound is produced rather than the material.

String

Musical instruments that produce sound from vibrating strings.
Main methods of playing - plucking, bowing, striking.
Broadly classified into 3 groups - lutes (support strings via a neck), harps (contain strings within a frame), zithers(strings mounted on a body).

Percussion

Musical instruments that are sounded by being struck or scraped by a beater / hand / another instrument.
Produce sound through vibration of entire body or when membrane is struck.
Broadly classified into pitched percussion instruments & unpitched percussion instruments.

Electronic

Musical instruments that produces sound using electronics.
The instrument might include a UI to control pitch, frequency & duration of each note. However, it is also common to separate it out into music controller & music synthesizer

Other

A class of various other devices that have been used as instruments in muscial compositions.
Some examples are vacuum cleaner and chainsaw

Overview of instrument type across recordings

There are 548 musical instruments captured in this data, across 2.1 million recordings. As can be seen, the Strings family has the most number of instruments at 195. One of the more unusual musical instruments is ‘vacuum cleaner’ that has been used in a musical composition as an instrument, and is classified here under ‘Others.'

String instruments have a dominating presence across recordings with almost 50% of the recordings having a string instrument.

Instruments by Gender

In many societies, musical roles are divided along gender lines: women sing and men play.

The dashboard below showcases the marginality of representation of women in the field of musical instruments. String instruments have the most representation at 10%. In contrast, women make up 30% of the vocalists, lending further credence to the claim “women sing, men play”.

When comparing countries on the percentage of women playing musical instruments, some interesting insights can be seen. For instance, in many ways, equality for women in the developed world has been more than in the developing one. If we consider an example of US and India; while representation of women is marginal in both, a larger percentage of women in India play string instruments. This is probably due to the close association of certain string instruments and vocal music, thereby inadvertently supporting the claim that “women sing, men play”.

Instruments that play together sound good together

Be it a few friends jamming in the basement or a symphonic orchestra playing, musical instruments are often played together, with each musical instrument family complementing the other.

To understand trends among the top 25 musical instruments, we looked at the frequency of occurrence of each with the others. The visualization below is dense since it takes into account only the top 25 instruments. However, within these, we can see that some instruments are played more with others. For example, keyboard and acoustic guitar. Also, among the top 25 musical instruments, there are five categories of guitars. If these were to be combined, then there would be a stronger connection to keyboards.

Geographical Spread of Musical Instruments

With 548 musical instruments being used, it is interesting to study the spread of these instruments across different countries.

For this visualization, we chose a random sample of about 100k entries to show the prevalence of an instrument across countries.

For this subset of data, we find that guitars are more used in Russia and Brazil. Saxophones are more prevalent in Italy, Poland and Bulgaria.

This visualization also shows the local appeal of regional musical instruments. For instance, instruments such as Basuri from India, Bangu from China and Samisen from Japan are limited to places of origin.
